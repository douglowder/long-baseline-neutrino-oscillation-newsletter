Thanks to solar neutrinos, EDF (Electricite de France) analyses the
mountain snow cover.

In snow-covered Pyrenees, a 6 meter high mast, equipped with thermal and
ultrasound sensors, transpierces the snow. Its name : NRC (Cosmic Ray
grader). Since october 2003, this weird apparatus, settled at 850 feet
high, close to the hydroelectric power plant of Orlu, plays a key role in
electricity management. It allows EDF to estimate with high accuracy the
level of water reserves and therefore evaluate its hydroelectric reserves.

The working principle is simple: "It computes the water content of the
snow cover in order to predict how much water will end in dams at the
time of snow melting" explains Guy Torrent, technical assistant at EDF
headquarters. In the past, we used to compute the water content with gamma
ray detectors. However, the radioactivity of Cesium 137 made the measure
unreliable.

Neutrinos emitted by the Sun are now the particles which are used to
compute the water content. Part of these particles are absorbed by water
molecules. A Geiger-Muller type of detector of half a meter squared area
records how much radiation was not absorbed and determines that way how
much quantity of water was crossed by Sun rays. Orlu's snow presently
contains "410 liters of water per meter cube", indicates Guy Torrent. The
apparatus also provides the density and snow thickness and allows one to
predict the melting time. "When the  measured density reaches 0.55, we
know that the process is irreversible and that snow is going to melt." he
explains.

Automatic and autonomous

After two prototypes were installed in 1997, 40 of the new generation
NRC's were put in place in French mountains. Completely automatic and
autonomous thanks to solar sensors, they allow to collect data, precise
at a 95 confidence level, in places which are difficult to access.
Continuously recorded, this data is transmitted by satellite to the
Prediction Center of Toulouse.
Coupled to measurements of river flows and pluviometers, they allow the
most precise prediction of the water quantity which is going to pour down
into
reserves before alimenting hydroelectric power plants, and finally
allow an estimation of the production capabilities of these plants.

This is a crucial evaluation since, once produced, electricity cannot be
stored.

While hydroeletric power plants provide only 15 percents of the French
