Long-Baseline news, November 2015

*** 2016 "Breakthrough Prize" for 5 Neutrino Experiments

1300 Physicists from Daya Bay, K2K/T2K, KamLAND, SNO and Super- Kamiokande won the Prize https://breakthroughprize.org/News/29 for collectively discovering & measuring nu oscillations. Naming all members of the collaboration for a prize is a departure from the way science prizes are traditionally awarded, and was done rather deliberately by the committee. Sergey Brin of Google, Anne Wojcicki of the genetics company 23andme, Jack Ma of Alibaba and his wife, Cathy Zhang, Yuri Milner, an entrepreneur and venture capitalist who founded the awards, and his wife, Julia Milner, and Mark Zuckerberg and his wife, Priscilla Chan, oversee the awards. http://www.fnal.gov/pub/today/archive/archive_2015/today15-11-09.html

*** LBNF/DUNE gets "Finding of No Significant Impact"

A DOE environmental study has determined that building & operating the proposed Long-Baseline Neutrino Facility and DUNE will not have a significant impact on the environment. DOE is following the National Environmental Policy Act of 1969, which requires that the environmental impacts of any federal project must be studied. DOE explored potential impacts in the Environmental Assessment (EA), including impacts on people and the environment, from building and operating the research machine. Impact to floodplains & wetlands were considered. http://news.fnal.gov/2015/10/page/3/

*** Who named the neutronino as the neutrino?

I once heard that Fermi had named the neutrino, so I repeated it as fact in my last newsletter. That's what underpaid journalists do. Luckily, readers correct me when the partial truth is incomplete. Pauli had called his particle a neutron. But to distinguish it from Chadwick's neutron, it needed a diminutive. In Italian, that would have been neutronino. The name was pronounced for the first time by Edoardo Amaldi, as he wrote in his 1984 very long and detailed Physics Report (Volume 111) in note 277: "The name neutrino, (a funny and grammatically incorrect contraction of "little neutron" in Italian: neutronino) entered the international terminology through Fermi, who started to use it sometime between the conference in Paris in July 1932 and the Solvay Conference October 1933 where Pauli used it. the word came out in a humorous conversation at the Instituto di Via Panisperna. Fermi, Amaldi and and few others were present and Fermi was explaining Pauli's hypothesis about his "light neutron". For distinguishing this particle from the Chadwick neutron, Amaldi jokingly used this funny name, - says Occhialini, who recalls of having shortly later told around this little story in Cambridge."

*** Citing the GENIE manual and reference

The manual for the GENIE neutrino simulation is on the arXiv at arXiv:1510.05494 . The primary reference for GENIE is the Feb 2010 NIM paper V614 p87. Only cite the user manual (in addition to NIM) if work depends on a feature of GENIE that is only documented there-in (reweighing/systematics, flux/geometry drivers and experimental interfaces, specialized event generation apps for specific experiments, atmospheric neutrinos, nucleon decay etc).

*** The MOMENT to search for CP violation

M. Blennow et al. write about a proposed MuOn decay MEdium-baseline NeuTrino beam that would operate with neutrinos from muon decay to study CP violation. The idea is based on a Gd-doped water detector proposed by Cao et al. [ arXiv:1401.8125 ] and requires 80% charge identification. arXiv:1511.02859

*** At 1 meter is it a long-baseline or short-baseline experiment?

Prospects for a sterile neutrino oscillation search using nu-N coherent scattering at 10-100 eV recoil threshold Si/Ge detectors is given in arXiv:1511.02834. This would be as near as 1 meter from a reactor core and sensitivities to 1 eV^2 at 0.01 amplitude.

*** Conceptual Design Report for IsoDAR@KamLAND

This idea uses an ion source and inverse beta decay for sterile neutrino and elastic scattering for NSI searches. arXiv:1511.05130 .
