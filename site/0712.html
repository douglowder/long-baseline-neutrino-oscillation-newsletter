Long-Baseline news, December 2007

*** UK budget cuts - but they continue Neutrino Research

The UK Science and Technology Facilities Council recently had to deal with a significant cut in research spending in the UK. One consequence was pulling out of the ILC. Here is what they wrote about neutrinos in their 2008-2012 Delivery Plan, "The recent discovery that neutrinos (a type of fundamental particle) have unexpected and unique properties, may ultimately account for the very existence of our Universe. UK researchers have played an important role in these discoveries. There are a range of opportunities for taking this area of science forward, including a programme of neutrino experiments building on the MINOS project at Fermilab in the USA, leading to the T2K experiment in Japan, due to come online in 2009; a programme of R&D for the SuperNEMO experiment, which will test whether neutrinos and anti-neutrinos are actually the same thing; and continuation of the MICE experiment which is addressing some of the technical challenges in the design and construction of a neutrino factory, which will fully uncover the neutrino.s properties. We will decide through the programmatic review our strategy for further investment in this area. The level of future funding will be dependent on the success of our restructuring plans." http://www.scitech.ac.uk/resources/pdf/delplan_07.pdf

*** US Budget Cuts,

The President, House and Senate each proposed a large increase for High Energy Physics spending in Oct 07-Oct 08 Budget, but couldn't agree on the rest of the budget. So HEP got a cut in an omnibus bill, passed 2 weeks ago. NOvA, ILC & ITER got ~$0. In the DOE Office of Science, the Bush plan was +16%, House +19%, Senate +18%, final +2.4%. For the National Science Foundation, Bush asked +8.7%, Senate +10.8%, House +10.0% and the final +2.5%.

*** NOvA gets 0 this year.

Ironically, NOvA had just jumped a hoop, passing its "External Independent Review". The EIR review found its cost & schedule are conservative and contingency is more than adequate, and planning "exemplary." They concluded that the project is ready to be baselined once findings are addressed. An effect of the cuts is an immediate stop of NOvA work at Fermilab, where staff will move to other projects. Fermilab is working to explore possible ways to mitigate problems. NOvA is not canceled. FNAL will work with the community, P5 and HEPAP to make a compelling roadmap that the DOE & congress will approve for next year.

*** No cuts in 08 running at Fermilab for MINOS, MiniBooNE

At a December 20 all hands meeting, FNAL's director says they will run the accelerator at planned levels in 2007-2008 for MINOS, MiniBooNE, SciBooNe ... Layoffs are being considered. http://vmsstreamer1.fnal.gov/VMS_Site_03/Lectures/AllHands/071220AllHands/index.htm

*** Fermilab, Steering away from the Iceberg

The effect and morale at Fermilab and other HEP groups in the US is pretty sour. Fermilab director Oddone has to make some tough decisions, but also will work with with administration and congressional officials to "steer the ship away from the iceberg." http://www.fnal.gov/pub/today/pdfs/2007/20071218.pdf - http://www.chicagotribune.com/news/local/chi-fermi_19dec19,1,2424810.story

*** GLACIER & other Liquid Argon detector R&D

There are several initiatives around the world to study the possible use of small, medium, large and gigantic liquid argon detectors for neutrino research. One is GLACIER or Giant Liquid Argon Charge Imaging ExpeRiment in Europe. A new page at the neutrino oscillation industry web site is devoted to liquid argon detector research. Additions/Corrections always welcome. http://www.neutrinooscillation.org/argon.html

*** Glaciers do make progress (or how to influence people)

Back in 1994, the Drell panel was charting the future of High Energy Physics after the demise of the SSC. The following is a portion of a letter that Bjorken wrote to the panel, "...I think the appropriate solution to this problem is for Fermilab to aggressively expand and diversify its scientific progress. Even glaciers are known to undergo surges, where the flow unexpectedly quickens for a while to an extraordinary degree...There is plenty of motivation for a long-baseline neutrino oscillation experiment, but it is caught in the glacier despite PAC/lab in- principle endorsement." MINOS, which was approved to eventually get 3.7 10^20 protons per year, has now passed 3.7 10^20 protons on target, and has accomplished many of its goals.
