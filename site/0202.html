Long-Baseline news, February 2002

*** Super-K Accident Update

They were planning to start draining the tank in February and this should only take a few weeks. When the accident took place, the amount of energy released was 75 MegaJoules. It registered on a seismographs 15 km away. An internal report on the accident is not yet available, but many calculations exist, e.g. http://tdserver1.fnal.gov/foster/BooNE_Photomultiplier_Implosion.pdf

*** Neutrino Mass 1.6 eV or so

The Sloan Digital Sky Survey is counting galaxies in clusters of galaxies and see too few. Neutrinos would lower the cluster abundance, so they fit to neutrino mass, and get a few eV, with best fit around 1.6. Omega-nu = 0.03 (Omega-matter ~ 0.3 today).

*** Neutrino Mass may not be .39 eV

The recent report on evidence for neutrinoless double beta decay is disputed by the 26 authors of a comment on that paper in hep-ex/0202018. They say that the extraction of the "signal" depends upon the choice of window and the absence of a flat background, among other problems pointed out in the paper. Also hep-ph/0201291

*** CNGS review at CERN

The Civil construction of the Cern Neutrinos to the Gran Sasso is going quite well, according to a recent review. Construction is done almost to the end of the decay tunnel. The extraction tunnel from the SPS to be shared with LHC is filling with racks. Modest cost increases are causing scrutiny, though. The approved cost was 71 MSF but is now thought to be 79 MSF. 29MSF is committed. There may be some other small cost additions as the designs of technical components are finished. CERN will soon re-evaluate and rank all non-LHC projects, including OPERA (Oscillation Project with Emulsion-tRacking Apparatus).

*** We get letters

"To Whom it May Concern, I just read about neutrinos in an astronomy book and I guess they are what I have been able to see falling since I was about 14. They look like empty spherical bubbles. Their edges are very thin and appear to be made of fuzzy light but they don't shine, just fall slowly. The exception to this rule is in stormy or pre-rainy rainy weather when the lights I see are more compact and whirl and dance. At these times they are solid - smaller than the bubbles but brighter. Sincerely RB"
