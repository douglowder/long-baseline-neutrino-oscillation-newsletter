Long-Baseline news, April 2011


Focusing on horns

*** Horn designer Simon Van der Meer, 1925-2011

At the 1963 Sienna Conference Simon van der Meer wrote, "In view of enhancing the fluxes of high energy neutrinos required for experiments, it is useful to focus towards the neutrino detector the pi's and Ks produced by protons interacting in the target. The statistically determined change in direction of the neutrino with respect to its parent pion or kaon will set a limit to the precision of focusing that is useful." A plasma device built by Lederman for BNL didn't work. Horns have gone on to enhance neutrino fluxes at labs around the world.

*** Horns at ANL

A horn was built for the nu experiment at ANL led by Telegdi, and then used for the bubble chamber. A 2nd "reflector" was built to increase the flux 30% based on calculations by then-postdoc Phil Schreiner. A power supply was ordered from Europe and arrived in the US but was destroyed when a truck took it under a bridge without enough clearance. Walter Prague then made a power supply on his own. Both horns operated for the rest of chamber's life.

*** Horns at Fermilab

A horn focuses as many pions as possible in the forward direction, to maximize the neutrino flux. The first MINOS horn simulations used NUADA, a program that has a "perfect focus" option. This put all pions parallel at the center of the beam. This is unachievable, but by comparing the neutrino flux to perfect focus, there was a meaningful figure of merit for any horn design. Jim Hylen has kept the NuMI/MINOS horns working, and he recalls, "The best horn story I remember is Frank Nezrick showed me an inner conductor they bent into an S shape (amazingly without breaking it) when the cooling failed on a horn test stand. Second best is the one I heard about them putting a bucket under the end of the horn at WANF, and periodically emptying it, after the water spray jet eroded through the inner conductor -- but the horn continued to run fine!"

*** Horns in Europe

In the 80's & 90's, Stephen Rangod worked with Jean-Marie Maugain on R&D at CERN allowed for replacing coaxial cables with strip- line bus bars for powering horns. This allowed avoiding the high tension on the fragile horns, and was a big step toward more reliable operation. In the 90s and into the 2000s, a fruitful collaboration was established between several labs interested in horn design, KEK, Fermilab and CERN, and later CNRS in France and others. This allowed injecting the latest developments into the horn design for CNGS. For example, the 7 m long inner conductor was built as one single piece, thanks to welding by electron bombardment. Using glass as an insulator and graphite as seals have also allowed reducing the cost of horn construction. A semi- rigid strip-line connection developed by CNRS Orsay and a water inlet coaxially inside the water outlet make the horn exchange somewhat easier, although this still remains a major operation.

*** South Pole Telescope search for neutrino mass

The Cosmic microwave background (CMB) was discovered accidentally in 1964 by Penzias & Wilson using a well-calibrated horn antenna. One device currently studying the CMB is the South Pole Telescope, a 10 m diameter wide-field telescope with a mm-wave bolometer camera. The data will be used to characterize the primordial matter power spectrum. A second-generation camera will measure the polarization of the CMB. From the analysis of the resulting polarization maps, the project will determine the angular power spectrum of the CMB B-mode polarization (curl component) induced by the lensing of the intrinsic E-mode polarization (curl-free component) by large-scale structures. The lensing B-mode spectrum provides sensitivity to the nu masses through their impact on the growth of the large-scale, lensing structure. arXiv:0907.4445

*** Stanislav Mikheyev, 1940-2011

Stanislav Mikheyev, an outstanding experimentalist and one of the inventors of the solar "MSW effect" died of heart attack on 23 Apr in Moscow, aged 70. Mikheyev was awarded the Bruno Pontecorvo Prize (2006, jointly with Smirnov and Wolfenstein) and the Sakurai Prize (2008, jointly with Smirnov).

*** ICARUS starts to fly

On 29 Mar, the Imaging Cosmic and Rare Underground Signals ICARUS experiment was officially inaugurated at the Gran Sasso Lab of INFN. http://cerncourier.com/cws/article/cern/45737 ICARUS is studying neutrino oscillations in the Cern to Gran Sasso beam (CNGS) as well as searching for other phenomena such as nucleon decay. It is the world's largest argon nu detector.

*** First results from T2K long-baseline neutrino experiment

T2K reported its 1st results on the search for excess e nus using 3.23 e19 of 1.45e20 protons on target. They have 2 single-ring e-like out of 33 fully contained events. They expect 1.3 with frozen cuts from the MC with oscillations and zero theta13. One event has a "decay" so there is one electron nu candidate. http://agenda.infn.it/getFile.py/access?contribId=25&sessionId=9&resId=0&materialId=slides&confId=3101

*** Tau neutrinos in atmospheric neutrinos from Super-K

They see a fitted tau excess corresponding to 213.6 events, which is 1.63 times the expectation based on oscillations. The data is inconsistent with no tau appearance at 3.8 sigma. See slide 12 of Jeff Wilkes' talk at Neutrino Telescopes 2011: http://agenda.infn.it/getFile.py/access?contribId=2&sessionId=3&resId=0&materialId=slides&confId=3101

*** NOvA far lab occupied in Northern Minnesota along NuMI beam

The University of Minnesota has taken possession of the NOvA Ash River Laboratory with the issuance of an Interim Certificate of Occupancy on 13 Apr. Next is to build the 15 kiloton nu detector.
