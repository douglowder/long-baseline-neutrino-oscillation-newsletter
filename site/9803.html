Long-Baseline news, March 1998

*** COSMOS withdrawn

With the loss of their emulsion target, COSMOS spokesman Bill Reay met Fermilab director John Peoples after which the COSMOS experiment was withdrawn. The collaboration had asked Fermilab if it was willing to get involved in the emulsion buying and scanning fronts, but as expected, this was not met with the requisite enthusiasm or funds. The Fermilab beam group is looking into how this will lower the cost of the near detector area. The COSMOS groups have been invited to join TOSCA at CERN.

*** MINOS lease and blasting

The MINOS experiment has now signed a long-term lease with the Minnesota Department of Natural Resources (DNR) which will allow the digging of the cavity towards Fermilab. Eight test blasts using up to 18 pounds of dynamite were set off to study the frequency response of various elements of the Soudan 2 hall and detector. Everything survived the blasting, including a module nearby with high voltage on. The MINOS cavity is now 0.1% done.

*** K2K beam pipe completed

K2K is on schedule for a January 1999 startup with the 12 GeV ps. No firm schedule yet exists for the proposed 50 GeV machine, but neutrino possibilities include further K2K running at higher energy, or LSND-type tests at the 3 GeV machine.

*** New MINOS review in response to Gilman panel

The Gilman report endorsed the long-baseline program at Fermilab and suggested a careful evaluation of the configuration of the facility; presumably the beam energy. Fermilab has assembled a panel headed by C. Baltay to review the physics of MINOS in May.

*** The Future: Neutrino to miss earth in 2028

According to calculations obtained by long-baseline news, one neutrino will pass within 30,000 miles of earth in October 2028, while another one will miss the earth by 600,000. We are fairly sure that neither one will pass through the asteroid 1997XF11.

*** The Past: Solar neutrino oscillations in 1969.

According to a 1969 issue of Scientific American on the solar nu problem, "...The most imaginative idea has come from the USSR, where Gribov and Pontecorvo have proposed that neutrinos have a kind of double identity: approximately half the time they are the ordinary `e nus' we suppose them to be but half the time they are `mu nus'. ... It will not be easy to test this unusual hypothesis, but it cannot be lightly dismissed.'' Several solar nu "popular articles" are at http://www.sns.ias.edu/~jnb/, which is available (of course) from the neutrino oscillation industry web page http://www.hep.anl.gov/NDK/Hypertext/nuindustry.html

*** OPERA web page linked to Neutrino Oscillation Industry

OPERA, "Oscillation Project with Emulsion-tRacking Apparatus". is a massive lead/emulsion target for a long-baseline experiment. It will search for numu to nutau in the parameter region indicated by the atmospheric neutrino anomaly. It exploits nuclear emulsion for the unambiguous detection of taus. OPERA will run at the LNGS in the future neutrino beam from CERN. See http://www1.na.infn.it/wsubnucl/accel/neutrino/opera.html

*** New EOI for low energy long-baseline from INFN

An EOI for a new long-baseline experiment has been submitted to Gran Sasso on Feb. 4th. Several INFN groups are considering a new experiment with a low energy beam for low delta m^2. With a 4 GeV (E-nu) beam, they get to 5 10^-4 eV^2 (superoptimistically?) assuming 100 events/kiloton/10^19 pot.

*** Palo Verde lab completed

The low background laboratory at Palo Verde has been completed. The neutron shielding water tanks are in place. The detector was being installed during February and March.

*** Taiwan reactor experiment TEXONO

The Taiwan EXperiment ON reactor neutrinOs will use low energy neutrinos as a probe for the first time. Using a 600 kg CsI pilot detector near the NPSI, II and IV reactors. If they reach their goal of 100 keV threshold, they will study low energy nu-e elastic scattering and search for a nu magnetic moment. The collaboration has several groups from Taiwan, China and the US.
