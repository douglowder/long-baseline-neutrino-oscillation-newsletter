Long-Baseline news, September 2001

*** Baton Rouge Neutrino Meeting

There will be an NNN (Next Neutrino & Nucleon Decay) Workshop with an emphasis on Experiments for a National Underground Lab; It's at LSU on December 10-12. For this and the next three meetings, see http://www.hep.anl.gov/ndk/hypertext/numeetings.html

*** Barcelona Workshop 9-11 November.

There will be a workshop on a massive underground neutrino detector with leptonic charge sign discrimination 9-11 November 2001 in Barcelona. The objective of the workshop is to investigate the feasibility and the physics reach of a very large detector with leptonic charge-sign discrimination, with a typical mass of 50 to 100 ktons, for studies of atmospheric and of long and very long baseline neutrinos from Neutrino Factories.

*** Japanese meetings on Neutrinos and Xenon detectors

An "International Workshop on Technique and Application of Xenon Detectors" will be held at University of Tokyo, Japan, on Dec 3-4. The purpose of this workshop is to discuss all aspects of experiments using xenon, including problems and techniques. The "International Workshop on Neutrino Oscillations and their Origin" will be held there 5-8 Dec. Initiated by the discovery of neutrino oscillations in atmospheric neutrinos in Super-Kamiokande, the series of the workshop entitled "Neutrino Oscillations and their Origin" has been held since 2000 in Japan.

*** Japanese association with neutrinos

Japan is responsible for the landmark experiment on atmospheric neutrinos, and crucial solar neutrino results. Japanese technology (emulsion) is also responsible for nu-tau detection. We anxiously wait as the nobel prize season approaches.

*** Controversy on Japanese events on MINOS Mural

The Cold Dark Matter Experiment (CDMS) shares the Soudan Lab with MINOS which is installing a grand mural with many neutrino references. The inclusion of some Super-K events has been controversial. CDMS will offer a smaller, yet complementary mural on their side, depicting a large, stick moose being chased by a pack of stick wolves. It won't represent history, or science, or anything profound at all. It will just warn people what is going on in the next cavern.

*** Neutrino Strike

A strike by Minnesota Public Employees has shut down the Soudan mine, where MINOS is being installed. The mine is operated by the state Department of Natural Resources. Jesse Ventura's smile couldn't stop the strike. Maybe his smile caused the strike?

*** Fermilab's Project Rescued

Despite the traumatic events of mid September, a review and rebaselining of the NuMI project took place at Fermilab. $33M will be added to the project cost, which now totals $171.4M, and the schedule was adjusted to take into account delays in civil construction of the NuMI beamline (which is now more than half done). First neutrinos are now expected in mid 2005.

*** OMNIS

The Observatory for Multiflavor NeutrInos from Supernovae is ready to turn in its $37M proposal. OMNIS concentrates on the neutral current interactions. Originally conceived as a 10 kT iron-scintillator sandwich, an alternate design using phototubes and 2 kTon of lead perchlorate is now favored. It could measure 3000 nue events, 25 nuebar and 325 numu+nutau events from a supernova at 8 kiloparsecs.

*** Neutrino Show

In Annecy (France) on 27 February 2002 at 19h there will be a dance show named "66 billions/cm2/second". Of course, the neutrino is the central part of the show. Notice a preference for theoretical value of the solar neutrino flux rather than for measurement.

*** First manmade neutrinos

Arguably the first manmade neutrinos were made at the CP-1 pile under Stagg Field at the University of Chicago Dec 1942-Early 1943. That event was remembered by many during the celebrations for Enrico Fermi's 100th birthday, 9/29/1. Lincoln Wolfenstein was an undergraduate there and walked along Stagg Field every day to get to his meal co-op. If he had known about the pile, he would have walked on the other side of the street. Is this a matter effect?

*** Tim Toohig

Tim Toohig, one of the first collaborators on the Fermilab to Minnesota long-baseline neutrino experiment, died unexpectedly while visiting SLAC on September 26th. An expert on tunneling at Fermilab and the SSC, he had planned to write up his SSC experiences in a book. He was well known as a catholic jesuit priest.
