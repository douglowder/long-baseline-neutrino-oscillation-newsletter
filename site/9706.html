Long-Baseline news, June 1997

*** CERN moves towards long-baseline beams

The SPS committee at CERN has favored the long-baseline option towards Gran Sasso over future intermediate baseline experiments in the JURA mountains. It is anticipated that much of the funding for the beam would be provided by Italy and others. "...in view of future competition, the neutrino beam (towards Gran Sasso should) be promptly constructed". This also implies that a future short-baseline experiment, TOSCA, would be in the same beamline and located in the near detector which is close to the airport. Long-baseline experiments (plural) at Gran Sasso are desired. ICARUS is clearly one, but more massive experiments were also thought desirable. CERN management has agreed. Individual proposals and further funding negotiations will have to be worked on before a real schedule is put forth.

*** Neutrinos from Hell (1)

The paper of Oyama et al (below) compares upward muons in Kamioka with high energy contained electron showers (>20 GeV). Since muons above this energy do not decay in the atmosphere before hitting the earth, and there are numus from pi decay, the numu to nue ratio at this energy is much greater than 2. That makes the measurement of this ratio very sensitive to numu->nue oscillations. They've looked, seen the expected ratio, and can rule out a large amount of the same parameter space that the reactor experiments are studying.

*** Neutrinos from Hell (2)

MACRO continues to have a strange angular distribution from its neutrino induced upward muons, with less muons than expected in the most upward bin, and a much smaller deficit at other upward angles. This effect, which was noticed in earlier data, has grown. The observed flux doesn't match the expected flux, but putting in oscillations, while improving the fit, still does not describe the data. A careful search for possible systematic errors has not led to a solution.

*** Neutrinos from Hell (3)

If the large mixing angle solution to the solar neutrino problem is correct, there is regeneration of solar nus in the earth. The "neutrino sky" would be brighter night than day.

*** Fire

A fire at the CERN SPS delayed this year's neutrino running of CHORUS and NOMAD by about a month, but the run time will be increased to make up for it.

*** Brimstone

Well concrete, actually, is flaking from top floors of Fermilab's high rise. $30M repairs are needed on the same timescale that MINOS construction funds are planned.

*** BOONE == BOOster Neutrino Experiment

BOONE is a new proposal at Fermilab to use a liquid scintillator detector together with a neutrino beam from Fermilab's 8 GeV booster. The goal would be to conclusively cover the entire region of parameter space suggested by the LSND experiment. A description of the project is available under "Neutrino Physics with the Fermilab Booster" at http://www.fnal.gov/projects/numi/workshop/summaries/summaries.html

*** Laughing at neutrinos

On their way from Fermilab to MINOS, neutrinos will pass under Walworth County Wisconsin. According to its newspaper "This Week", dairy farmer Rick Getka laughs at the thought of neutrinos and said, "Now if I'm walking along and I feel these little vibrations, I'll know what's going on." Their reporter wrote that "Most of us will never even know the neutrinos were here. Totally harmless, they will head for their big clash with the thousand-ton iron detector, not even stopping to pick up a postcard of dairy cows along the way."

*** Science funding report in Japan --- Japan Hadron Project???

According to the June 13 1997 issue of Science, the Fiscal Structure Reform Council wants to pare the Japanese budget deficit from 5.4% to no more than 3% GDP by 2003. A previously announced 5-year 17-trillion-yen plan to increase R&D levels 50% would be stretched out per their recommendations. The report "leaves a question mark hanging over.. the Japanese Hadron Project" which would upgrade the long-baseline K2K project from 12 GeV and low statistics to 50 GeV and high statistics.

*** Hello from Greece

This last month, a message pointing out that the report on the Nestor deployment was incomplete was inadvertently broadcast to the whole list, together with several pages of mail errors. The settings of the list have been adjusted to make this less likely from now on. Meanwhile, a mention of Greek surnamed physicist Tom Ypsilantis in long-baseline news was found by a solid state physicist of the same name from Australia, leading to contact between these two distant cousins, and prompting Tom to remark that he didn't know long-baseline news had a personals column.
