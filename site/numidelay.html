Delays plague Fermilab project

 

By Heather Vogell

Tribune staff reporter

July 1, 2001

 

Already 18 months behind schedule, an ambitious high-energy physics experiment at Fermi National Accelerator Laboratory lost yet more

time as an investigation into workplace accidents halted construction last week.

 

With the experiment also over budget, some scientists worry the delays will give foreign competitors an edge in the race to make the latest discovery into the elusive

subatomic particle, the neutrino.

 

Neutrinos are so small, yet so ubiquitous, that a million are passing through any given gallon of space at any moment. There are so many that some cosmologists suspect

they collectively have as much mass as all the stars.

 

Though it exists virtually everywhere, the ghostly particle is so hard to study that Fermilab is digging an underground tunnel a mile long to beam the particles from here to

Minnesota just to get a good look at how they behave.

 

Different groups of physicists are vying to show definitively that neutrinos have mass and thus play a key role in the working of the universe.

 

Yet 15 months after it began, the project has run into trouble. Scientists said Fermilab's problems have become a topic at lunch tables and conferences throughout the

country, where some worry about competitors such as CERN Laboratory in Switzerland. A similar experiment is scheduled to begin there in May 2005.

 

"The U.S. is holding its own right now, but certainly CERN is always a threat to surpass us," said Robert Park, a physics professor at the University of Maryland and

director of public information for the American Physical Society.

 

A deeper understanding of one of the universe's fundamental building blocks is not all that's at stake. The reputation of the American science program abroad and

congressional support for physics projects at home also feed off such successes.

 

"This is important to the United States, this is not just a soccer game," Park said.

 

With its price tag climbing to nearly $168 million, Fermilab's so-called NuMi project is about 20 percent more costly than originally estimated.

 

But Fermilab Director Michael Witherell said workers' safety is the most pressing issue right now.

 

"At this point, we're concerned about safety and we'll worry about schedule later," Witherell said.

 

The U.S. Department of Energy this week sent a team of experts to Fermilab to launch a monthlong investigation into a recent construction accident.

 

Steven Lumbert, 53, of Montgomery in Kane County was drilling an air shaft when a piece of equipment snapped loose and struck him in the head. After spending a

week in a critical care wing, he was listed in good condition last week at Delnor Community Hospital in Geneva.

 

The energy team will also look at 10 other accidents that have caused workers to miss days on the job since the project began, officials said. The probe, termed a "Type

A" investigation, is the most comprehensive form reserved for serious accidents, said Jeff Sherwood, an Energy Department spokesman.

 

A representative from the construction project's lead contractor, S.A. Healy, declined to comment on the investigation.

 

Construction restarted Friday after a weeklong stand-down.

 

The scientific community is eager to get on with preparations for the experiment, which aims to map out new territory in the subatomic world.

 

Most of the time, neutrinos zip through space without paying much heed to anything. Their failure to interact with other particles has made them hard to pin down, and for

years, scientists thought they had no mass at all.

 

Advances in the last three years provide good evidence that neutrinos have an infinitesimal mass that causes them to transform--or oscillate--between three forms, which

scientists call "flavors." There are electron, muon and tau neutrinos.

 

Several laboratories have studied neutrinos coming from the sun, the atmosphere or from particle accelerators. But in late 2004 or 2005, according to a revised

schedule, Fermilab hopes to run the first experiment in which neutrinos are created in a controlled setting and travel hundreds of miles before being measured again.

 

For the study, the particle accelerator at Fermilab will manufacture a stream of muon neutrinos and send it through the Earth to an old iron mine in Soudan, Minn., 450

miles away.

 

An instant (0.0025 seconds) later, a detector in the mine will take a second snapshot of the particles.

 

Physicists hope to get better data on neutrinos' oscillation, and therefore, their mass, opening up new doors for theories of the universe. The results will complement those

of previous experiments in laboratories in New Mexico, Switzerland, Canada and Japan.

 

"It's a huge puzzle and each one of us goes out and gets a little piece," said Janet Conrad, a professor at Columbia University and researcher at Fermilab.

 

The findings could also have significance for scientists peering into space.

 

Cosmologists want to know how much of matter believed to exist in the universe, but yet to be accounted for, can be attributed to neutrinos. Such mystery matter

explains the force of gravity that keeps galaxies from spinning apart.

 

Stars make up only a half a percent of all matter.

 

"We think that neutrinos, based on previous experiments, contribute at least a half a percent," said Michael Turner, chairman of the astrophysics department at the

University of Chicago. "So they're at least as important as stars."

 

Witherell said cost overruns came in two major areas. Original estimates were too low both for the construction of the tunnel and two caverns, and for the beam line that

the neutrinos pass through before heading to Minnesota.

 

The delays are tied to the overruns, and Fermilab plans to request additional funding from Congress for fiscal year 2003--the year the experiment was originally

scheduled to take place, he said.

 

"In some sense, it's not surprising that when you tackle such large, difficult, novel projects, that one of them might have some unexpected costs ... and schedule delays,"

Witherell said.

 

"We are doing things that haven't been done before; it's different from just building a building."
