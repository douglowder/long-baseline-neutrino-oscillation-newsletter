Long-Baseline news, September 2002

*** New neutrinos - MiniBooNE starts run

The MiniBooNE experiment at Fermilab has started taking data to confirm or refute the numubar to nuebar signal from LSND. They are looking for numu to nue oscillations using primary beam from the Fermilab Booster. With the start of beam come the first neutrino events. They identified neutrinos that created ring-shaped flashes of light in 250,000-gallons of mineral oil.

*** New approval for ICARUS long-baseline experiment

The CNGS neutrino beam from CERN to Gran Sasso is being built, and the ICARUS 600 ton detector will be installed early next year at Gran Sasso. Now Proposal CERN/SPSC 2002-027 (P-323) has been recommended by the CERN SPSC. "The Committee congratulated the Collaboration for the very successful operation of the T600 module demonstrating very long tracks and excellent performance. The Committee appreciated the proposed use of the CNGS beam and recommends the experiment for approval in consultation with the Grand Sasso Scientific Committee." They asked for details on timelines, manpower and finances to run by 2006.

*** New (low energy) Solar Neutrino Experiments - why they are needed

1. >99% of the predicted spectrum lies below 1 MeV. 2. Do unique tests of the simplified scenarios that we work with by testing them at low energies instead of 8B neutrino energies. We need CC and nu-e scattering experiments. 3. Measure mixing angle better. 4. Test for CNO neutrinos. (Hans Bethe thought they would be dominant, but Bahcall's model says only 1.5% of the luminosity.)

*** New Spokespeople for Neutrino Factory Collaboration

Steve Geer (Fermilab) and Bob Palmer (Brookhaven) were elected co-spokespersons for the Nu Factory/Mu Collider Collaboration.

*** New Minoan

MINOS collaborator Tass Belias is the happy father a a baby girl born on 6th September in Herakleion Crete (once ruled by MINOS).

*** A comment that might not be useful.

Asked whether there would be a positive signal from MiniBooNE, Gordon Kane said, "I don't think there is any theorist who can make a useful comment."

*** A comment on Fermilab and NuMI from the US House

"The (Science) Committee is concerned about the difficulties being experienced with the luminosity upgrade of the Tevatron and with the Neutrinos at the Main Injector, both projects at Fermi National Accelerator Laboratory. The Committee expects the Department and the laboratory to exercise aggressive project management to bring these projects back on schedule, and to do so within the funds available for High Energy Physics." But NuMI excavation is complete, and the most recent milestones are ahead of schedule. A contract for Service Buildings & outfitting has been awarded with a 13-month schedule for completion.

*** A Comment for Neutrino Physics from a US President

According to columnist George Will, former US President Ronald Reagan said "Throw Deep" when approving the SSC. 350 Scientists met at the Washington Hilton last month and mentally changed it to "Go Deep" as plans for a National Underground Physics Lab were discussed at the NeSS meeting (for Neutrinos and Subterranean Science). While the main focus was on neutrinos, the far ranging meeting discussed other opportunities from proton decay to national security to sub-surface microbiology.

*** NFAC input

The Neutrino Facilities Advisory Committee (to the National Research Council) has met to draft its final report. They were asked for input on ICE-cube and the National Underground Lab, but reportedly will also comment on the scientific merit of many other neutrino and underground physics initiatives. See www7.nationalacademies.org/bpa/projects_neutrino_committee.html

*** Nobel poll

Nobel prizes come out in October, and it should be inevitable that neutrino oscillations be the justification for one or more prizes. Should they give one year's prize for solar neutrinos and another one for atmospheric neutrinos? Or just 1? With 3 MNS angles, this newsletter would like to report 2 prizes soon and 1 later. Could Davis and others get a Chemistry prize? Super-K and SNO have both been great. Whoever wins, our new understanding of the neutrino is based on worldwide efforts and talent.
