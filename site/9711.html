Long-Baseline news, November 1997

Long-Baseline Neutrino News November 1997

*** CHOOZ result

The Chooz neutrino oscillation reactor experiment sees no evidence for a change in the neutrino energy spectrum. The measured nu rate 1.1 km from the reactors is 25.5 +- 1./day The background is 1.1 +- 0.25 /day. The ratio between measured and expected flux is R = 0.98 +- 0.04 (stat) +- 0.04 (syst). This pretty much rules out numu to nue as an explanation of the atmospheric neutrino anomaly. It also rules out the Acker/Pakvasa solution and the maximal mixing solution with the previous fit of delta m-squared.

*** LSND continues to have effect with Tungsten target

Preliminary results from LSND with a Tungsten target continue to show an excess of events consistent with numubar to nuebar oscillation. Previously they had used a segmented water target. The excess from the 1996-97 data is 30.3 +- 14.8 and from all data it is 82.8 +- 23.7 events, corresponding to P=.31%.

*** CHORUS takes last event

After four years of operation the data acquisition for CHORUS at CERN ended on Nov. 10, 1997. The first event in the emulsion was taken on May 5, 1994.

*** NESS: Neutrinos at the European Spallation Source

The European Spallation Source will exceed the neutrino intensity of the ISIS facility by more than a factor of 60. In conjunction with a 1000 ton detector under the beam stop, the achievable sensitivity for numbar to nuebar oscillation is 6 10^-5 in mixing. A link to NESS is now on the neutrino oscillation industry page under short-baseline; www.hep.anl.gov/ndk/hypertext/nu_industry.html

*** Long-baseline neutrino experiments at muon colliders

Muon colliders with a straight section in the recirculating arm are prodigious neutrino sources. There will be measurable rates from a hypothetical muon collider at any point on earth that one could aim the beam. For example, a 250 GeV muon beam could make 10^5 neutrino events per kiloton year at Gran Sasso. Also, this would be a good source of high energy electron neutrinos.

*** Neutrino Radiation at muon colliders

Muon colliders will produce a high enough flux of neutrinos that the muons that they create are a potential radiation concern. A muon collider put underground with 250 GeV muons which exits the earth 10 km away leads to an annual dose at the surface which is 3% of the Fermilab limit, which itself is 1/10 the federal limit. This is not a problem, but the dose increases as E^3, so muon colliders much above 1 TeV would have to be prohibitively deep or deal with this problem some other way.

*** NuMI project at Fermilab

With a picture of the Soudan mine's Whitey on the cover, Ferminews gave the NuMI project and neutrino physics some prominence. On October 13th, President Clinton signed the DOE appropriations bill, which includes $5.5M for NuMI and makes it a bona fide project. Other proposals at Fermilab are now looking towards the day when NuMI funding is over. But the Main Injector had a setback when they tested the cooling water lines and there were major leaks. Since the NuMI project near detector hall will be the lowest point on the site, we will expect the leaks to be fixed before neutrinos flow towards Soudan (or they'll be wet).
