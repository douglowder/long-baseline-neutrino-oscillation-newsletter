Long-Baseline news, December 2003

*** APS Neutrino Study

A hastily convened workshop attracted over 100 participants to kick off the study on the future of neutrino projects in December. Six working groups were established to write reports, and input is solicited from the entire neutrino community. The Groups and Leaders are:
1. Solar and atmospheric nu experiments - J. Bahcall & J. Klein
2. Reactor neutrino experiments - G. Barenboim & E. Blucher
3. Superbeam experiments/development - W. Marciano & D. Michael
4. Neutrino factory and beta beam experiments and development - S. Geer & M. Zisman
5. Neutrinoless double beta decay and direct searches for neutrino mass - S. Elliott & P. Vogel
6. What cosmology/astrophysics and neutrino physics can teach each other - S. Barwick & J. Beacom
For transparencies: http://www.neutrinooscillation.org/studyaps/

*** JPARCnu approved again

Reversing an earlier setback, the Japanese cabinet agreed in December to approve the neutrino program (JPARCnu) starting next fiscal year. The decision is based on the recommendation from the November review on J-PARC. The input from the International Advisory committee, which labeled the neutrino program the #1 priority, was very important. The final official decision will be made at the end of March at the House level. The total cost for the neutrino program is 16 billion Yen. Thus, the total budget for J-PARC is now increased from 135 billion Yen to 151 billion Yen. The first-year budget (JFY04) for neutrino is 0.6 billion Yen, and the total construction period is 5 years.

*** ANITA Launch

ANITA (ANtarctic Impulse Transient Array) is a new Balloon-Borne HE Nu Detector. A mission (TIGER) launched from Antarctica's McMurdo base 16-dec-03 was successfully brought down near Mawson base 4-jan-04 after an 18 day flight. On board, the ANITA team flew a small prototype instrument, ANITA-LITE, to understand more about the radio background in Antarctica and begin a search for the highest energy neutrinos known. The goal of the ANITA experiment is to listen for sharp bursts of radio waves emitted when a high-energy cosmic-ray neutrino interacts within the ice.

*** What Shape is Ice-Cube?

Physicists from any of the eleven institutions in the big ten are not allowed to answer this question. IceCube is a 1 km^3 high -energy neutrino observatory being built and installed in the clear deep ice below the South Pole Station. There will be 4980 PMT's on 80 strings in a project more hexagonal than square. The startup phase has been approved by the National Science Board and funds allocated to start in 04/05. Full construction at 16 strings per year will take 6 years. Each hole in the ice is .5$M.
