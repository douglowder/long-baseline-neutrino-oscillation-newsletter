Long-Baseline news, December 2008

*** J-PARC accelerates to 30 GeV

The J-PARC 50-GeV Synchrotron (MR) has successfully accelerated the proton beams and extracted them to a beam dump. The first beam commissioning started 22 Dec 08. Next day, the beam was accelerated to 30 GeV, the target of the first stage of J-PARC, and was extracted to the abort beam dump intentionally. The MR is the 1st large accelerator with an imaginary transition energy. Preliminary results show that small beam loss occurred in the first few 100 ms, then no significant current drop observed until the extraction. The acceleration time was 2.5 seconds. The next step for the MR is the extraction to the hadron hall (Jan 09) and to the neutrino beam line (Apr 09).

*** ANITA-2 launched

The ANITA-2 payload launched 21 Dec in calm conditions. All systems are functioning well as they started taking RF triggers with the receivers on within 8 hours. The ANtarctic Impulsive Transient Array (ANITA) is designed to study nus with E ~ 1e18 eV by detecting radio pulses from interactions with the Antarctic ice sheet. This is to be accomplished using an array of 32-40 radio antennas (cylindrically arranged with an approximate radius of 3m and a height of 5-7m) suspended from a helium balloon. http://www.phys.hawaii.edu/~anita/

*** MiniBooNE nu_e appearance result with antineutrinos

MiniBooNE previously showed its neutrino results, but recently showed their results with 3.386e20 pot focusing for antineutrinos. http://theory.fnal.gov/jetp/talks/karagiorgi.pdf Their goal is 5e20. Using a blind analysis for nu_mu to nu_e antineutrino oscillations, the nu_e data was in agreement with Monte Carlo Background. From 475-1250 MeV, 61 events with 57.8 +-10 background. No strong evidence for oscillations, limited by statistics. There is also no evidence for the low energy excess, ruling out some explanations of the excess in neutrinos.

*** Should Neutrino Telescopes look up or down?

Individual neutrinos carry no stamp of origin to distinguish whether they are produced as secondaries of cosmic-ray interactions in the atmosphere (atmospheric neutrinos) or by interactions of high energy particles in distant astrophysical sources. It is generally assumed that the only signature of an astrophysical origin would be a significant number from the same direction or in coincidence with an event such as a GRB or a significant excess of high energy neutrinos above the flux of atmospheric neutrinos. But Schonert et al. show they can suppress downward atmospheric neutrinos by vetoing the muon for energies > 10 TeV with an efficiency > 99%. ( arXiv:0812.4308
