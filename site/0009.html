Long-Baseline news, September 2000

*** OPERA long-baseline experiment approved at CERN

The CERN SPSC Committee considers the OPERA experiment to have a potentially large impact on the understanding of neutrino oscillations. They recommended P318/OPERA for approval with the condition of a number of technical questions like aging and temperature stability to be clarified, and a list of milestones and an appropriate time schedule be established in common by the SPSC and LNGSC referees and in consultation with the Collaboration.

*** ICARUS/ICANOE loses NOE part

ICARUS is building a 600 ton liquid argon detector. The ICANOE proposal was to interleave ICARUS modules with a solid target/scintillation fiber calorimeter. Currently the T600 module is being equipped at Padua and will be operated in 2001. The SPSC congratulated ICARUS on its progress and awaits long tracks in the calorimeter. It is still a Neutrino Oscillation Experiment (NOE) but without the solid target.

*** Gold cheap -- Homestake mine to close

This was found at http://biz.yahoo.com/rf/000911/n11520519_2.html . The historic (but inefficient) mine where solar neutrinos have been studied for over 20 years is closing due to low gold prices. Homestake, the oldest continually listed stock on the New York Stock Exchange, said it would wind down operations of the 124-year-old mine over 16 months. It is the company's namesake mine, and the experiment is located about twice as deep as Soudan or WIPP. KGF, a gold mine in India where proton decay and deep muons were studied in the 70's and 80's closed several years ago.

*** Goldstone - Neutrinos hitting the moon

The Goldstone project is planning to study the highest energy neutrinos which hit the moon by looking for radio emission. They have finished several runs using NASA/JPL's Goldstone Deep Space Station near Barstow, California, using a 70 m telescope (DSS14) to provide a trigger. They require a coincidence offline in a 34 meter telescope (DSS13) which is 22 km away. This separation is large enough to suppress terrestrial sources, and small enough so they both lie within most lunar Cherenkov cones. See http://www.physics.ucla.edu/~moonemp/public/index.html or the neutrino oscillation industry web page under "Nu Telescopes".
