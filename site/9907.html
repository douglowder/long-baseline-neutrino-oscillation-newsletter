Long-Baseline news, July 1999

*** MINOS undergroundbreaking

In one of his first acts as Fermilab Director, Mike Witherall helped break wall for the MINOS laboratory at Soudan for the new long-baseline experiment. (see photo at http://www.hep.anl.gov/ndk/hypertext/numi.html) His instructions to the collaboration were "Just go do it and tell us what the answer is." There was a videotape from Congressman Oberstar, and representatives of DOE, NSF and the University of Minnesota. Dating herself to at least the late 1970's, UMinn Vice President for Research Dr. Christine Maziar said, "We Golden Gophers do it underground."

*** Final Results - Chooz reactor neutrino Experiment

No evidence for nu-ebar disappearance. 90% limit .10 (mixing) at high mass, 7 10^-4 eV^2 at large mixing. See Apollonio below.

*** Data from SAGE Solar Neutrino Experiment

8 years data gives 67.2 (+7.2-7.0)(+3.5-3.0)SNU, half the SSM. Oscillation fits are shown. astro-ph/9907131 and astro-ph/9907113

*** ICARUS + NOE = /I canoe (can you canoe?)/ICANOE

A freak July windstorm hit the Soudan/Boundary Waters Canoe area. Soudan operations were not affected, but 12 million trees fell over, hampering canoers. At about the same time in Europe, the ICARUS and NOE collaborations joined forces for the CERN/NGS long-baseline neutrino proposal which is due in September. Much of the design work (and the name) is preliminary.

*** New nus from mu mus amuse.

The highlight of the Lyons neutrino factory meeting was a wine tour of Beaujolais. That caused a real coming together of ideas, both on the machine and the physics sides. At the meeting, 3 schemes became one, from the target downstream, with only the proton driver being different. From 24 R&D areas, 4 were selected for high priority investigation, targeting, pi and mu capture, phase rotation and cooling. The possible access to CP violation in very long-baseline neutrino oscillations was the most exciting prospect but it would be difficult to disentangle from matter effects. Nu physics is pushing the machine parameters to very high intensities (10^22 mu decays per year) and energies (50 GeV). Neutrino experiments very close to the source were also exciting. Very compact, high resolution detectors would have neutrino event rates (4.10^7 /kg/year) comparable with those previously available in e and mu beams. Transparencies are available at http://lyoinfo.in2p3.fr/nufact99/

*** Isotropic neutrino beams; or at least 2 Dimensions

In 1990 I submitted an LOI for a neutrino beam from FNAL to Soudan. Fermilab was having similar discussions with representatives from IMB, Grande and DUMAND. Taiji Yamanouchi accepted the LOI, shook his head, and said that maybe Fermilab should just produce an isotropic neutrino beam. That idea almost makes sense now. At the FNAL user's meeting, Chris Quigg described a muon storage ring aimed at Super-K, AMANDA, KGF, Alice Springs and Gran-Sasso.

*** Three dimensional Atmospheric Neutrino Flux Calculation

Previous calculations of the atmospheric neutrino flux have made a 1-D approximation, i.e. secondaries and decay products are collinear to the primary cosmic ray. A new 3-D calculation (see Battistoni et al, below) shows that the 1-D approximation gives the wrong neutrino angular distribution for sub-GeV neutrinos. However the angular smearing from measuring only the lepton is large enough to wash out this difference, so there is no bias in determining oscillation parameters in current experiments. An upcoming paper will address comparisons will balloon mu data.

*** Neutrinos in extra dimensions

There is recent theoretical interest in the notion that extra dimensions could be as large as 1 mm, but have escaped detection. The true Planck scale would then be a few TeV, rather than 10^19 as it appears in our 4 dimensional world. This would solve the hierarchy problem. If the right-handed neutrinos exist in these extra dimensions, a Dirac neutrino mass of 10^-3 eV naturally emerges. For example see Smirnov hep-ph/9904211

*** Tube switch

The last of the 1220 phototubes from LSND has been removed and boxed up. The phototubes will be shipped to Fermilab in the fall, tested in FY2000, and installed in FY2001. BooNE data taking should begin by the end of 2001.

*** The jellyfish dip?

Tokyo power reduced 3 1.1 million KW reactors to .8 million KW because a swarm of jellyfish blocked its pipes at the Kashiwazaki- Kariwa nuclear plant. If KamLAND was running (it isn't yet), it would have seen this dip in neutrino production.
