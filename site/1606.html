Long-Baseline news, June 2016

*** MINOS+ winds down

This newsletter started in 1992 as part of what became MINOS. The MINOS/MINOS+ Far Detector saw its last beam neutrino on 29 June 2016. The NuMI beam for MINOS had been commissioned 03 December 2004. It provided 2.6194 e21 Protons on Target at 120 GeV, It saw atmospheric neutrinos for one full 11 year solar cycle. The Near Detector becomes part of Minerva for all practical purposes. Analysis of MINOS+ data continues. On 16 Jun, MINOS/MINOS+ collaborations invited attendees of the Users Meeting to an "End-of-MINOS" reception that followed the last session.

*** NOvA winds up, 700 kW test for Main Injector

The design for NOvA is for running with the 120 GeV proton beam with a 700-kilowatt beam power. On June 13 the accelerator conducted a brief but successful test with the Main Injector running at 700 kW. The projection for FY17 (Nov-Jul) is >5e20 pot. http://news.fnal.gov/2016/06/main-injector-reaches-700-kilowatts/

*** Too many neutrino physicists?

The Neutrino 2016 meeting about to start in London had to cap the number of attendees at 700 to respect the fire regulations of the Royal Geographical Society. It is probably a record. There were 460 poster abstracts, and only 400 could be accommodated. The Public Lecture was way oversubscribed. Since people were turned away from the waiting list, the sessions will be videoed subject to agreement of the speakers; available @ neutrino2016.iopconfs.org.

*** Do we know that the neutrino is Majorana?

arXiv:1605.02975 says yes. But the answer is no. You shouldn't divide by zero.

*** Do we know the masses of the three neutrinos?

No. ArXiv:1605.03159 says the heaviest is 63+-11 meV and the other 2 are 40+-18 meV for either ordering. Based on an upper limit on the sum of the neutrino masses of 290 meV from cosmology, it assumes a box distribution for that number with an error using an incorrect analogy to wire planes.

*** Do we know the ordering of the neutrino masses?

Not yet, but S. Hannestad and T. Schwetz show how to interpret the answer to this question from cosmological data. Current data gives 2:1 odds for normal, based on a 95% upper limit on the sum of neutrino masses of 140 meV. To get 95% on the order, an accuracy better than 20 meV on the sum of the neutrino masses is needed. See arXiv:1606.04691.

*** CHANT - CHerenkov from Astrophysical Neutrinos Telescope

This is an idea for a space-based system of atmospheric Cherenkov telescopes with a very wide field of view. It would study the astrophysical neutrino flux from 10 PeV to 10 EeV. It is described in A. Nerenov et al., arXiv:1606.03629
