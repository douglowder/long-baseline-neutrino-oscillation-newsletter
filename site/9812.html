Long-Baseline news, December 1998

*** Neutrino is the particle of the year

Long-Baseline Neutrino News feels that the confirmation of neutrino mass by the Super-Kamiokande collaboration justifies considering the neutrino to be the particle of 1998. This story was ranked #4 for 1998 by Science Magazine.

*** JHF status

The JHF 50 GeV accelerator has strong new support from Monbu-sho and Science and Technology Agency (STA) in Japan. It will likely be constructed at the site of Japan Atomic Energy Research Institute (JAERI) as a cooperative project between KEK and JAERI. Last year the project sought a supplemental JFY98 budget to get started, but it was concluded that it was too early. A unified KEK/JAERI proposal for JHF plus a test experimental area for transmutation is being written with a second stage being a higher flux accelerator. The present hope is to start the joint project from JFY2000 and to have beams in 2005.

Occasionally fiction makes its way into long-baseline news, such as the October 1998 item on JHF. This happens with rumors. But there is real fiction: Watching the movie "Star Trek - Insurrection" this holiday, my movie companion asked me, "How does a tricorder detect neutrinos?" *** John Bahcall wins National Medal of Science

Established by Congress in 1959, the award is bestowed annually by the President for outstanding contributions in the sciences. John Bahcall, from the Institute for Advanced Study at Princeton University, was given the medal for his pioneering efforts in neutrino astrophysics and his contributions to the development and planning of the Hubble Space Telescope.

*** Ken Young Memorial Fund

We're sad to hear of the death of neutrino researcher Ken Young from the University of Washington. A memorial fund at the University of Washington will be used to encourage physics-related topics of special interest to Ken (innovative teaching, etc.) Send contributions to Linda Rose, box 351560, University of Washington Dept. of Physics, Seattle WA 98195.
