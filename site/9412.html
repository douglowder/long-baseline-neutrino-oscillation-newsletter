*** Long Baseline Collaboration Meetings; Reference Detector

At the December Long Baseline collaboration meeting, a reference detector was specified for the purpose of costing and physics results in the proposal. The reference detector is as follows: 8 kt iron plus 3.85 kt concrete, or about 12 kt total mass. The concrete is 8m x 8m square with 5 cm thick plates and a 2cm gap for 480 layers of detector. The steel is 8m wide octagons with a .3m diameter hole. Layers are 4cm thick with 2cm gap for 480 layers of detector (960 layers total). The concrete section is 28.8m long and the steel 33.6 m long for 62.4m. Active elements will be limited streamer tubes with 2cm (x8m) strips. Soudan 2 will sit where it is, and the near detector will have both concrete and steel sections, but the size has been under further discussion.

*** KEK Long baseline plans

At the 2nd KEK workshop, there was a strong consensus that a Long Baseline Experiment is one of the most important things that the KEK PS can do. They need to do a number of things to get ready, most of them requiring about 3 years, thus sometime in 1998 they might start running. The PS has 5 x 10^12 ppp. If they get all the beam and the cycle time is 2 s, they can get 10^20 protons in 2 years, which would yield 400 events in Super-K. About 1/3rd of the steel plate liner is installed, with PM's due next spring.

*** Gallex reports source experiment--They get what's expected.

The Gallex collaboration has announced the result of a test of their neutrino experiment using a 51-Cr neutrino source on their 71-Ge production rate. They report 1.04 +- 0.12 for R=Q-measured/Q-calculated. After 20 years of solar neutrino observations, it's a relief that one of them has been calibrated with a neutrino source. It is noted below that this result can place a limit on nu-e disappearance above 0.2 eV^2.

*** Neutrons suggested as solution to atmospheric anomaly

Is the apparent numu to nue ratio due to pizeros created by neutrons from cosmic muons which create hadronic showers in the rock? This is the suggestion of a new preprint by Ryazhskaya. She argues that R increases with depth in the data, charged pi's undergo nuclear interactions and fall below Cerenkov threshold, and that veto counters don't veto neutrons. Arguments against this idea include different depth and rate dependences at IMB and Kamiokande, the fact that veto counters should also veto the muon that goes along with the neutron, and zenith angle and distance to the wall distributions look like isotropic neutrino induced distributions and not downward going neutron induced distributions.

*** Upward going muon calculations need to consider low energy cross sections.

Do the upward going muon and stopping muon rates limit neutrino oscillations? Even if one believes the Volkova flux, the answer may be no, according to a new paper by Lipari, Lusignoli and Sartogo. They point out that that the lowest multiplicity exclusive channels are an important part of the neutrino cross section, and they do not scale with energy in the same way as deep inelastic scattering. They calculate a 10% lower value of stopping muons to throughgoing muons than used in the IMB limit based on this test. The statistical error is 12%, so the limit would be reduced. Together with the potential for background in the upward going stopping events, there is no limit at all left at 90% CL.

*** DOE cutbacks and Long Baseline

Recently it has begun to look like all four long baseline projects will proceed (Fermilab, Brookhaven, KEK, CERN). On December 19th, it was announced that the Department of Energy would get some rather large cuts (subject to Congressional approval). $10B in 5 years. DOE has a total budget (95) of $18.5 billion. Even if HEP is not affected directly, we will be indirectly. ANL-HEP had a hard time getting discretionary funds this year due to funding cutbacks in nuclear energy research.
