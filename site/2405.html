*** T2K and Super-K joint fit

In arXiv:2405.12488, T2K and Super-Kamiokande present a 1st joint measurement of atmospheric and beam neutrino data. Using 3244.4 d of atmospheric nu data and 19.7(16.3)e20 pot in nu(antinu) mode, they measure neutrino oscillation parameters. The results show an exclusion of CP-conservation at 1.9-2.0 sigma, limited preference for the normal ordering, and no strong octant preference for q_23.

*** Neutrinos and the moon

Over 30 years ago, Ken Lande pointed out that a long-baseline neutrino beam from the SSC to a detector on the moon would sample a region of L/E parameter space comparable to the suggested value which would explain the solar neutrino problem. (He knew about the problems at the time, and soon there was one more.) In a year with a widely-viewed eclipse and a blue moon, recent papers suggest other ways to exploit the moon for particle physics.

*** Lunar antineutrinos and heat

In arXiv:2406.00882, Dye and Barma estimate the fluxes of heat and antineutrinos within the moon. They find 302 GW of radiogenic heating in the crust and an additional 185 GW in the mantle, and nu fluxes of 2.02e6 and 6.40e5 cm^(-2)s^(-1) respectively.

*** Neutrino Gravitational Wave Memory from SN using the moon

In arXiv:2405.13211 K. Gill describes the possibility of seeing GW linear memory, a phenomenon resulting from a combination of aspherical matter ejection and anisotropic nu emission during stellar collapse - using gravitational wave detectors on the moon.

*** The moon - the final frontier for proton decay

Underground detectors searching for evidence of GUTs inspired nucleon decay have to deal with ~100/year/kiloton background events from atmospheric neutrinos. This background doesn't exist on the moon. In arXiv:2405.15845 , S. Baum et al. show a competitive sensitivity from using a 100 g paleo-detector sample from the moon to search for evidence of p -> nu K+ tracks.

*** SBND finds the booster beam

Using a run for which 111 photomultipliers were on, during the night of 8-9 May, SBND measured 876 events out of 8291 beam spills with an uptime of ~40 minutes. In the first ever "top-hat" plot, (i.e. events versus time with respect to the beam spill), they clearly saw the 1.6 microsecond structure of FNAL's booster beam.

*** Eos Neutrino Detector Tests Begin

Called Eos, for the Titan goddess of dawn, the detector is a 10-m- tall, 5-m-wide cylinder filled with water & organic scintillator. It will combine scintillation and Cherenkov emission to better detect neutrinos, for both nonproliferation & fundamental physics. http://news.berkeley.edu/2024/03/15/tests-begin-on-sensitive-neutrino-detector-for-nonproliferation-as-well-as-physics/ and https://nuc.berkeley.edu/eos-neutrino-detector-tests-begin/
