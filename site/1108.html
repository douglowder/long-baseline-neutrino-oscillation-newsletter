Long-Baseline news, August 2011

*** MINOS antineutrinos now oscillate like neutrinos

In a previous now published analysis of 1.7e20 pot, MINOS saw an antineutrino disappearance result which didn't overlap well in parameter space with the neutrino result. MINOS did the right thing and pressed for more antineutrino running at NuMI. With 1.2e20 additional pot, a careful new analysis shows that neutrinos and antineutrinos are now consistent. The previous result appears to have been a fluctuation. Currently, 273 antineutrino events are expected with no oscillations, and 193 are seen, excluding null-oscillations at 7.3 sigma. The best fit is now to an antineutrino delta msquared of 2.62 +0.31 -0.28 e-3 eV^2 and square of twice the mixing angle > 0.75 at 90% CL. Neutrino and antineutrino are consistent at 42% CL. For details, see http://theory.fnal.gov/jetp/talks/MINOS_AntiNu_Sousa_2011.pdf .
Also see arXiv:1108.1509 for antinus in the nu beam.

*** Delta msquared goes down with new measurements?

It has been noticed (Kamioka-Super-K, Soudan 2, MINOS neutrino, MINOS antineutrino) that early measurements of delta msquared are often replaced by more accurate values that are lower. Is this just the fact that things are discovered with upward fluctuations?

*** Update on the Indian Neutrino Observatory (INO) project

INO involves the construction of an underground laboratory and surface facilities near Pottipuram village in Tamil Nadu, and the construction of a 50 kton magnetized Iron calorimeter (ICAL) to study neutrinos, together with an INO center at Madurai and a graduate training program. ICAL uses Resistive Plate Chambers, prototyped at TIFR in Mumbai. It is scheduled for six years to completion in 2017. At the site, the environment and forest clearances have been obtained, and formalities for transfer of 26 Hectares of land at Pottipuram and 12 Hectares at Madurai are complete. They are waiting for the transfer of land by the Tamil Nadu Government.

*** DOE Intensity Frontier Workshop 30 Nov- 2 Dec in Rockville Md

A DOE sponsored workshop will take place at the Rockville Hilton near DC on 30 November to 2 December. It will examine the physics program for High Energy Physics in the U.S. as facilities transition from the energy frontier to the intensity frontier. There will be at least five working groups; (nucleons, nuclei & atoms; heavy quarks; charged leptons; neutrinos; photons) and organizers for neutrinos will include Kate Scholberg, Sam Zeller, Andre De Gouvea and Kevin Pitts. A goal is for the working groups to get considerable work done before the workshop, so feel free to contact these people to help. A web page is imminent.

*** Where is the Higgs?

At the 2011 lepton photon meeting, the name of the local area network for participants to check their email was "Higgs". Other than that, however, the Higgs was nowhere to be seen, with some limits presented from ATLAS and CMS. The CERN director argued that the non-observation of the Higgs would be a discovery. Hints reported from earlier EPS meeting, didn't resolve themselves for this non-collider physicist. Would the lack of a Higgs mean that the neutrino doesn't have a Dirac mass? Of course not. Whatever is giving a Dirac mass to the other particles could do so for nus.

*** I believe a non-zero theta-13 has been seen by T2K and MINOS

As evidenced from the discussions at NuFACT, most experimental nu physicists seem to believe that a value of theta_13 accessible to the current and future round of experiments has been observed, and are making their plans accordingly. Some people, particularly theorists, say this is "not good enough". One example is Michael Peskin, who gave the summary talk at Lepton-Photon, who argued that the 3.2 sigma global fit did not meet his criterion of "5 sigma discovery" often mentioned by collider physicists. Fortunately, it doesn't affect experiment what Michael Peskin thinks and most collider physicists don't believe their own rhetoric. A 5.8 sigma difference in CP parameters between neutral and charged B decays wasn't called a "discovery", it was called a "puzzle". A theorist said "no-one should publish effects under 2 sigma". My immediate comeback was we should publish whatever we see, but I now wonder about the following value of "x": "No theorist should publish anything unless there is an experimental effect of at least x sigma." My first observation is that x is zero, but I'm not even sure that negative values are frowned upon.

*** Want to get in a debate about statistics?

Louis Lyons' book "Statistics for nuclear and particle physicists" has the following quote after the title page, "The result of this experiment was inconclusive, so we had to use statistics." Many things that I've heard people say about statistics seems wrong to me. Upon reflection, this also applies to most things that I've ever said about statistics. Yet somehow as a discipline, we seem to get things right, in the sense of taking seriously results that turn out to be true, and being skeptical about results that are not. I've been involved in several discussions lately, and I've summarized some of the points in a made-up discussion at lepton photon 2011: http://www.neutrinooscillation.org/statistics.html
