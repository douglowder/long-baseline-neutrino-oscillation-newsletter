Long-Baseline news, December 2022

*** Mary Bishai new DUNE cospokesperson

With Regina Rameika moving to the Department of Energy, the new cospokesperson of DUNE is Brookhaven's Mary Bishai, (see https://www.bnl.gov/staff/mbishai ). She had previously served as the staff scientist of the Long-Baseline Neutrino Experiment. She now leads DUNE together with Sergio Bertolucci.

*** Hitoshi Murayama to chair P5 process, report due October 2023

The membership of P5 was announced at HEPAP in December, including the cochair Karsten Heeger. Neutrino physicists on the 29 member panel include Mayly Sanchez, Patrick Huber, Kendall Mahn & Jelena Maricic. Four town halls (FNAL, SLAC, LBNL & BNL) will occur from January to April, plus virtual town halls focusing on early career scientists. A preliminary report is due Aug 2023. His HEPAP slides: https://science.osti.gov/-/media/hep/hepap/pdf/202212/Hitoshi_HEPAP_202212.pdf

*** Neutrinoless double beta decay white paper

This document, arXiv:2212.11099 prepared for the Fundamental Symmetries, Neutrons, and Neutrinos Town Meeting related to the 2023 Nuclear Physics Long Range Plan, has been endorsed by the major collaborations and many theorists. They include CUPID, KamLAND-Zen, LEGEND, Majorana, nEXO, NEXT, SNO+, SuperNEMO, and THEIA. It recommends the timely construction of experiments at the ton scale using multiple isotopes, and a robust research program, as well as a diverse R&D program, exploring ideas with sensitivity beyond th inverted mass ordering.

*** AQUADOM, Daya Bay, RENO, Double Chooz and SNO

https://www.usatoday.com/story/news/world/2022/12/17/berlin-aquadom-us-manufacturer-investigate/10916162002/ Large acrylic vessels hold neutrino experiments and they hold aquariums filled with fish (but not both together). The December failure of AQUADOM, thought to be the largest cylindrical tank in the world, was a spectacular reminder of what's at stake. The spherical SNO chamber, built by U of Washington, successfully held a priceless amount of deuterated water for many years. Daya Bay, RENO and Double Chooz had concentric acrylic tanks, and the three experiments near nuclear reactors provided the first measurements of the neutrino mixing parameter theta_13. In Double Chooz, there was some observed leakage between the gamma catcher and buffer liquids. See the Double Chooz Detector paper at https://link.springer.com/article/10.1140/epjc/s10052-022-10726-x No fish were harmed in the conduct of any neutrino experiment.

*** Rant - Neutrinos do not oscillate as they travel; P(osc) does

Neutrino oscillations are a consequence of quantum mechanics, but are often described wrongly, even by physicists. If a neutrino starts as a muon neutrino at point A and is measured to be a tau neutrino at point B, it isn't correct to say that the change happened in between. They interact as flavor states and propagate as mass states. Neutrino oscillations are the result of mixing, not transmutation. I wince at language, such as appeared in this month's CERN Courier article on KM3NeT, that "As they whiz through the universe, ..., they have the unique ability to oscillate between their three different types of flavors."

*** Is it time to remeasure the half life of 71Ge?

The short answer is yes. It was last measured in 1985 to be 11.43 +- 0.03 days by Hampel and Remsberg, PRC31, 666, but previous measurements with larger errors varied. Giunti et al. in arXiv:2212.09722 discuss how the Gallium anomaly depends on it. A value of 13.5 +- 0.5 d would cancel the anomaly.

*** J. Robert Oppenheimer and Neutrinos

https://www.npr.org/2022/12/17/1143896431/j-robert-oppenheimer-security-clearance Now that Oppenheimer's security clearance has been restored, it is interesting to speculate if his work on neutrinos might continue. In 1931, Carlson & Oppenheimer speculated that neutrinos (they called them neutrons) with a magnetic moment might be responsible for cosmic rays observed in cloud chambers with low ionization. Phys. Rev. 38, 1737 (1931) & 41, 763 (1932). They concluded, "...there is no experimental evidence for the existence of a particle like the magnetic neutron." Since he passed away in 1967 further progress seems unlikely. On a similar note, when the prolific author Isaac Asimov died, I knew it would slow down his rate of publishing books, but I didn't think it would stop him.
