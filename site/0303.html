*** One year of Neutrinoless Double Beta Decay

In hep-ph/0302248 & hep-ph/0302237, H.V. Klapdor-Kleingrothaus says that evidence for 0nu beta-beta decay of 76Ge reported in late 01 by Heidelberg-Moscow (still operating) which uses 11 kg of 86% enriched 76Ge detectors, and is the most sensitive such experiment worldwide, was indirectly supported recently by analysis of an earlier experiment using natural Germanium detectors. The 16 events observed in the Heidelberg-Moscow experiment, from which a 1/2-life of 1.5x10**25 years was deduced at 95% CL, correspond to an expectation of 0.6 events in the California experiment by David Caldwell and colleagues. While in the latter the same background lines were found in the vicinity of the double beta signal, no indication of a double beta signal, which should show up as a line at 2039.006 keV, was found, consistent with the Heidelberg-Moscow result.

*** Pontecorvo and nuclear reactors in Russia

Pontecorvo may be best known for his prediction in 1958 that neutrinos would oscillate if they had mass, and that those oscillations would be characterized by a mixing matrix. He also suggested that neutrinos were detectable from both reactors and the sun. When he suggested to Fermi that they try to see nus from the sun, "he (Fermi) understood that it was a long range project, so why waste time with that?" Pontecorvo also did precise tritium and muon beta decay experiments, and determined that the muon must decay to an electron and two undetected particles. Pontecorvo's politics and the times he lived in shaped his career. Because he kept his involvement with the French Communist party a secret from Harwell, he was unhappy there, even before they discovered the truth. During the Korean war, Pontecorvo emigrated to the USSR, since he "did not want to be in the west when World War III broke out". Unfortunately, once in the USSR he was not allowed to do experiments near reactors, because it was thought he might be a spy from the West.

*** Nestor's First Floor

Nestor's 1st floor (twelve-15" tubes) of a tower was successfully deployed at the deep-sea site (4000 m) in the Ionian Sea. The floor is connected to the shore station in Methoni by a 28 km cable. Control and read-out systems have been checked out and seem to be operating correctly. Rates ~0.5 Hz are consistent with expectations at the present trigger settings. The program of calibration and evaluation of the detector has now started.
