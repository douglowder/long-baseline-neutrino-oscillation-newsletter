Long-Baseline news, July 2019

*** Nishikawa fest at KEK 27 Sep 2019 13:30-17:30

Koichiro Nishikawa (1949-2018) pioneered long-baseline neutrino oscillation experiments as the founder of the K2K experiment and the first spokesperson of T2K. He has been recognized for his contributions as Director of the KEK Institute of Particle and Nuclear Studies, Deputy Director of J-PARC Center and a professor at Kyoto University. A symposium to celebrate his career will be 13:30-17:30 27 Sep, at KEK Tsukuba Campus. There is no fee. https://conference-indico.kek.jp/indico/event/81/. From 18:30, a gathering in his memory will be at Hotel Grand Shinonome, with a 10,000 yen fee. A bus will be arranged. Register by 30 Aug. This symposium follows the J-PARC Symposium, 23-26 Sep 2019.

*** ANNIE installed at Fermilab

The Accelerator Neutrino Neutron Interaction Experiment (ANNIE) is now installed at Fermilab and beginning its commissioning process in preparation for beam this fall. ANNIE will perform a detailed measurement of neutron yields from nu-nucleus interactions in H2O. This measurement will aid in better understanding reconstruction of E_nu for future long-baseline neutrino analyses and also in developing neutron tagging for proton decay and Diffuse Supernova Neutrino Background (DSNB) searches. In addition, ANNIE will be the first neutrino experiment to demonstrate newly commercialized Large Area Picosecond Photodetectors (LAPPDs) and the first demonstration of Gadolinium-loaded water on a neutrino beam.

*** MiniBoone turned off for the last time

At 11 am on Wednesday 17 July, the MiniBooNE detector was turned off for the last time. It had run for many years in the Fermilab Booster beam with many interesting results on nus and Dark Matter.

*** RANT - Effective neutrino masses are not masses

A new newsletter feature will be rants about a nu physics topic. I've done this before, but more rants are coming, now with labels.
---Direct mass & 0nu2beta experiments will measure or limit a mixture of nu masses & mixing angles which are called effective masses, or sometimes the nue mass. Each one is a parameter that the experiment can measure that has units of mass & is related to the 3 actual masses. But the number is not the mass of anything. It is not an m that you can put into the vacuum Schrodinger equation for the nue. The nue does not have a mass. If you try to conserve both E & p, you violate the uncertainty principle.

*** Change in Planck's constant, h

The uncertainty principle depends on Planck's constant which has a new value in May 2019. Actually h didn't change. It's constant after all, or at least we think it is. What changed, slightly, was the definition of the units that it is made up of. h is now exactly 6.6260693e-34 joule-s. You know this wasn't driven by neutrino physicists. We would have defined hbar = h/(2 pi). In the 2017 RPP it was given as 6.626070040(81)e-34 J-s.

*** Neutrino mass upper limits from cosmology

Many cosmological nu mass bounds assume degenerate masses, which is not a good approximation. Choudhury & Hannestad have updated sum(m) limits separately for different orders in arXiv:1907.12598. 95% C.L. upper limits are 145 meV(normal), 170 meV(inverted), and 122 meV (degenerate). Lower limits come from oscillations.
