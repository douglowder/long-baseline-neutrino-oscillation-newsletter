Long-Baseline news, January 2015

*** ELBNF LOI submitted to Fermilab PAC

http://www.fnal.gov/directorate/program_planning/Jan2015Public/LOI-LBNF.pdf The global neutrino community worked together to prepare this letter of intent to pursue an accelerator-based long-baseline neutrino experiment, as well as neutrino astrophysics and nucleon decay, with an approximately 40-kt (fiducial mass) modular liquid Argon TPC detector located deep underground and a high-resolution near detector. A goal is the deployment of the first 10-kt detector on the timescale of 2021, followed by expansion to the full detector size as soon as possible. 527 signed the LOI. https://indico.fnal.gov/confRegistrantsDisplay.py/list?confId=9090

*** First ELBNF collaboration meeting

There were 278 registrants for the first collaboration meeting of the Experiment at the Long-Baseline Neutrino Facility (ELBNF) at FNAL on 22-23 January. Slides are available at https://indico.fnal.gov/conferenceOtherViews.py?view=standard&confId=9209 The institutional board met and endorsed the LOI and started the process of establishing a collaboration structure.

*** Last LBNE institutional board meeting

On 28 Jan, the previous LBNE collaboration's institutional board met and voted to turn over its assets to ELBNF and dissolve. The former LBNE project will transition from one project to two; a Long-Baseline Neutrino Facility and the detector. When this has progressed, DOE has asked for a "CD-1 refresh" to understand the new scope.

*** Double Chooz catches up with its name

Double Chooz was the first reactor neutrino experiment to see a deficit due to theta-13, but it started with just a far detector. Now the Near Detector is running and it really is a "Double" Chooz. Ironically, the Chooz Far Detector is behind the security fence, since it was near the site of a decommissioned underground reactor. And the Near Detector isn't behind the security fence!

*** Juno storms ahead. (as named)

Weathermen in the U.S. have started naming winter storms, and the record-breaking storm that dumped 3 feet of snow on Boston was called Juno. Sorry about that. It won't happen again. Meanwhile the Juno reactor nu experiment in China laid a foundation stone for a new lab for a large detector to measure the hierarchy. U.S. members are considering a Pelletron based calibration system. A 2019 data start is the goal. Approval will likely depend on a China contribution to the U.S. program at a commensurable level. http://articles.economictimes.indiatimes.com/2015-01-11/news/57941080_1_neutrino-chinese-scientists-lab

*** NESSiE changes its name to NESSiE

The January Fermilab PAC meeting heard from NOvA, ELBNF (or P1062), the Short-Baseline Program including LAr1-ND, MicroBooNE and ICARUS, Captain-BNB, Captain-Minerva, Annie and NESSiE. https://indico.fnal.gov/conferenceDisplay.py?confId=9287 The NESSiE Collaboration proposed a spectrometer in the Booster beam to determine disappearance behavior at the 1 eV mass scale. It stands for Neutrino Experiment with SpectromenterS in fErmilab. The last E used to stand for Europe. Since OPERA has started to dismantle its spectrometer this January, a call has been opened by INFN to reuse it, but time is tight for a decision.

*** NOvA status

A retrofit of NOvA to replace some faulty cooling hoses was completed in November at which point routine operations with 14 kt began. Uptime is routinely >95%. Exposure so far is about 2e20 pot for the full detector mass, or about 1/3rd of a TDR year. First results are expected before this summer with an update around the time of the next shutdown, ~fall 2015.
